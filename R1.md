### Rebuttal
Thank you for the thoughtful and constructive feedback. We appreciate the recognition of the practical importance of the problem, the empirical performance across different corruption settings, and the long-horizon analysis. Below we respond to each point.

### (W1) Limited novelty and engineering contribution

We thank the reviewer for recognizing the practical aspects of our work. However, we would like to clarify that our contribution goes beyond an engineering combination of existing techniques.

In the low-data regime, we find that standard self-supervised models (e.g., Noise2Noise) consistently show catastrophic overfitting (Fig. 1 (a)). Existing regularization can delay this effect, but does not prevent performance degradation or improve the final result (Fig. 1 (b), (c), (d)). This behavior is closely related to **over-parameterization and noise memorization**.

We then study pruning and find that it helps reduce over-parameterization and suppress noise memorization, but introduces a critical trade-off that **over-pruning degrades representation capacity**, while **under-pruning fails to suppress noise memorization** (as shown in Fig. 7).

This observation suggests that **capacity control alone is insufficient to ensure robust learning**.

The regularization term plays a complementary role by enforcing **prediction consistency across noisy inputs**, which helps recover signal structure and stabilize training, particularly in high corruption regimes (as shown in Fig. 10).
These findings lead to our key insight that **effective mitigation requires the joint action of capacity control and self-consistency regularization**, which is not immediately evident from prior work.

Therefore, SHIELD-PR is motivated by our analysis, rather than a straightforward combination of existing techniques. To the best of our knowledge, this interaction between model capacity and overfitting in low-data self-supervised settings has not been explicitly studied before.

More broadly, our results suggest a **general training principle for self-supervised learning under extreme data scarcity**: controlling model capacity and enforcing prediction consistency during training are both necessary to prevent noise memorization and ensure stable convergence (as shown in Fig. 1).

We believe this perspective provides insight beyond the specific implementation and may inform future designs in related frameworks. Importantly, we validate this behavior across multiple self-supervised frameworks (e.g., N2N and Neb2Neb), suggesting that the proposed principle is not tied to a specific model, but reflects a more general phenomenon in low-data self-supervised learning.

### (W4) Scalability to larger models (with experimental evidence)

We thank the reviewer for raising this point.

As noted, the current lightweight U-Net architecture (~1M parameters) was a **standard design choice** of the N2N model. To address your concern, we conducted additional experiments to evaluate the performance of SHIELD-PR on **larger networks**. Specifically, we increased the depth and number of channels of the U-Net to create larger architectures, and compared Dense and PR variants. We reported PSNR/SSIM on in-distribution (InD) and out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets. We refer the reviewer to (https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SHIELDPR/rebuttal_table.md) for Table A.

From these results, we observe:

- **PR consistently improves performance over dense baselines**, regardless of model size. 
- Increasing model capacity improves absolute performance for both Dense and PR models, but **Dense networks overfit more quickly**, while PR stabilizes training and prevents degradation.
- Even for the largest model tested (1.5M parameters), SHIELD-PR achieves **robust gains in both InD and OOD scenarios**, demonstrating that the method is **not restricted to lightweight architectures**.

These experiments confirm that SHIELD-PR’s effectiveness is **orthogonal to model size** and that **capacity control via pruning remains critical** to prevent overfitting, even as the network scales. We include these results and discussion in the revision to clarify scalability.
